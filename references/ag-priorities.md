# AG Enforcement Priorities — What Each State Actually Pursues

**Purpose.** Statutory penalties are a ceiling, not a forecast. The practical risk profile is shaped by which AG is actually enforcing and what they are prioritizing — both as articulated in public statements and as observed in actual enforcement actions. This reference catalogs each state AG's stated and observed priorities so risk-scoring can weight by *actual* enforcement posture, not just *theoretical* penalty exposure.

> **Caveat.** AG priorities shift more frequently than the statutes themselves. Administrations turn over; enforcement initiatives launch and conclude; sweeps target specific industries and then move on. Verify against current AG announcements for any high-stakes engagement.

## Risk-tier summary

| Tier | States | Characteristic |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Most aggressive** | TX, CA (CPPA + AG), CO | Active AG initiatives; multiple announced enforcement priorities; multi-million-dollar settlements |
| **Tier 2 — Substantial active** | CT, FL, MD (post Oct 2025) | Public reports / clear priorities; meaningful settlements |
| **Tier 3 — Established / moderate** | VA, OR, MN, NJ, MT | Active programs but lower profile / fewer headlines |
| **Tier 4 — Developing** | UT, IA, IN, KY, NE, TN, NH, DE, RI | Newer regimes; enforcement track record still developing |

## California — CPPA + AG

**Enforcement infrastructure:** Two parallel enforcers — California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA, established under CPRA) and California Attorney General. The two coordinate but operate independently.

**Stated priorities (CPPA 2023-2025 enforcement plan and announcements):**

1. **Connected vehicles.** July 2023 announcement of an industry-wide investigation into connected-vehicle data practices — geolocation collection, in-cabin audio capture, sharing with insurers and data brokers, disclosure adequacy.
2. **Data brokers.** Implementation of CA's data-broker registry (§ 1798.99.80 et seq.) and enforcement of registration requirements.
3. **GPC handling.** Universal opt-out preference signal recognition, especially for advertising-driven sites.
4. **Sensitive PI handling.** "Limit Use" right operationalization; sensitive PI flows beyond § 7027(m) safe-harbor purposes.
5. **Minors.** Sale and sharing of personal information of known minors; targeted advertising to known minors.
6. **Automated decision-making (ADMT).** New rulemaking finalized 2024-2025 for ADMT in employment, housing, insurance, healthcare, education contexts.

**Observed enforcement (illustrative):**

- *In re Sephora* (2022): $1.2M — sale not disclosed, no DNS link, GPC not honored.
- *People v. DoorDash* (2024): $375k — data cooperative as "sale" without disclosure.
- *In re American Honda* (CPPA, 2024): $632.5k — verification excessive, opt-out symmetry, processor contracts inadequate.
- *People v. Tilting Point Media* (Cal. AG + LA City Attorney, 2024): $500k — sale of minor data without parental consent.

**Practical implications:**

- CA is the highest-volume state enforcement jurisdiction. Expect scrutiny.
- CPPA is more focused on operational mechanics (verification, processor contracts, GPC handling) than headline data sales. The AG focuses on bigger fact patterns (Sephora, DoorDash, Tilting Point).
- CPPA's first enforcement actions targeted operational implementation — *Honda* in particular signaled that "compliance theater" (privacy notice ≠ actual practices) will be tested.
- Industry sweeps are an emerging CPPA technique. Connected vehicles in 2023; data brokers ongoing; expect more.

## Texas — TDPSA + companion enforcement

**Enforcement infrastructure:** Texas Attorney General. Notably aggressive across multiple privacy-relevant statutes.

**Stated priorities (Tex. AG public materials, June 2024 enforcement initiative announcement):**

1. **Privacy notice content** — particularly for large platforms.
2. **Sensitive-data handling** — biometric (under CUBI), health, child data.
3. **Minor protections** — TDPSA's known-minor provisions plus parallel SCOPE Act enforcement against social media platforms.
4. **Data broker activities** — TX has a Data Broker Registration framework.
5. **AI / facial recognition** — biometric privacy is a core focus area.

**Observed enforcement (illustrative):**

- *Texas v. Meta* (CUBI, 2024): $1.4 billion settlement — the largest privacy settlement under any state biometric privacy law.
- *Texas v. Google* (CUBI / DTPA, 2024): $1.375 billion settlement — facial geometry, voiceprints, geolocation, incognito-mode tracking.
- TDPSA enforcement initiative (June 2024 onward): broad investigatory demand letters to large platforms, SCOPE Act enforcement against TikTok, X, Discord, others.

**Practical implications:**

- TX AG has the highest-profile state-AG privacy enforcement program in 2024-2025.
- The AG combines TDPSA, CUBI, DTPA, and the SCOPE Act for compounded damages exposure — controllers should not view TDPSA in isolation.
- TX AG enforcement extends beyond statutorily-applicable controllers — the SBA-size carve-out under TDPSA does not eliminate exposure under CUBI, DTPA, or the SCOPE Act.
- Settlement velocity: TX AG has settled large matters quickly, suggesting active investigation pipeline rather than one-off cases.

## Colorado — CPA, rulemaking-focused

**Enforcement infrastructure:** Colorado Attorney General + District Attorneys (Colo. Rev. Stat. § 6-1-1311(2) authorizes DAs to bring action for state residents within their jurisdictions).

**Stated priorities (CO AG public guidance and rulemaking history):**

1. **UOOM recognition.** CO maintains a published list of recognized UOOMs (currently includes GPC). Compliance with the published list is a stated priority.
2. **Profiling for legal or significant effects.** CPA Rules § 9 on profiling rights and DPA requirements.
3. **DPAs (Data Protection Assessments).** CPA Rules § 8 — the most prescriptive DPA requirements in any state.
4. **Loyalty programs.** CPA Rules § 6.04 — unique loyalty-program disclosure rules.
5. **Sensitive-data consent specificity.** CPA Rules § 7 — anti-dark-pattern rules.

**Observed enforcement:**

- CO AG has emphasized rulemaking and compliance-engagement letters over headline-grabbing public enforcement actions.
- Cure period sunset Jan 1, 2025 — enforcement now without statutory cure right.

**Practical implications:**

- CO Rules are the most prescriptive state privacy regulatory text in the US. Controllers operating in CO must read the rules, not just the statute.
- CO's lower-key enforcement style is in part because its rules guide compliance more aggressively than other states' do; a controller compliant with the rules is unlikely to attract enforcement attention.
- Multi-state controllers often align to CO rules as a higher-water-mark across states.

## Connecticut — CTDPA, public reporting

**Enforcement infrastructure:** Connecticut Attorney General.

**Stated priorities (CT AG enforcement reports 2024):**

1. **Privacy notice content.** Most-cited issue in compliance reviews.
2. **Sensitive-data consent.** Sensitive-data processing without proper opt-in consent.
3. **Sale / cross-context behavioral advertising disclosure** and opt-out availability.
4. **Processor contracts.** Adequacy of controller-processor contractual terms.
5. **Dark patterns.** Friction asymmetry between opt-in and opt-out flows.

**Observed enforcement:**

- CT AG has published enforcement reports identifying common violations encountered during compliance reviews. This is unusual — most AGs do not publish "what we look for" guides.
- Cure period sunset Jan 1, 2025.

**Practical implications:**

- CT AG's enforcement reports give controllers a direct roadmap of what gets cited. Use them to prioritize remediation specifically against the named violation patterns.
- Pattern of violations in CT is consistent with what surfaces in CA and CO — same fixes apply across states.

## Florida — FDBR, narrow but harsh

**Enforcement infrastructure:** Florida Attorney General (Department of Legal Affairs).

**Stated priorities (Fla. AG announcements):**

1. **Minor protections.** FDBR and parallel HB 3 (Online Protections for Minors) enforcement against social platforms.
2. **Large platform compliance.** FDBR's $1B threshold targets the Big Tech footprint specifically.
3. **Voice-assistant and smart-speaker data.** FDBR's specific activity prongs include voice-assistant operators.
4. **App store / digital distribution platform conduct.** FDBR's third activity prong.

**Observed enforcement:**

- TikTok and other large platforms under investigation under FDBR + minor-protection statutes.
- Penalty multipliers for minor-related violations make FDBR's effective penalty-per-violation the highest in any state ($150k tripled).

**Practical implications:**

- FDBR's narrow applicability means most controllers don't directly worry about it. Those that do (large platforms, voice/smart-speaker operators) face the most severe per-violation exposure of any state.
- Even for non-FDBR-applicable controllers, Florida's parallel minor-protection statutes can reach social-media-adjacent services.

## Maryland — MODPA (effective Oct 2025)

**Enforcement infrastructure:** Maryland AG (Consumer Protection Division).

**Stated priorities (anticipated based on the substantive law):**

1. **Sale of sensitive data** — MODPA's flat ban (§ 14-4607(b)(1)).
2. **Minor data sale and targeted advertising** — flat ban for known under-18 (§ 14-4607(b)(2)).
3. **Data minimization** — MODPA's "reasonably necessary and proportionate" standard, the strictest minimization language in any state.

**Observed enforcement:**

- Effective date is October 2025. Initial enforcement is expected to focus on the flat-ban provisions, since these are unambiguous violations.

**Practical implications:**

- MODPA's substantive standards are the strictest in the nation. Controllers should not assume that "comply with CA / CO" satisfies MD — the flat bans require behavioral changes.
- MD is likely to be a future Tier 1 jurisdiction once enforcement begins.

## Virginia — VCDPA, established but lower-profile

**Enforcement infrastructure:** Virginia Attorney General.

**Stated priorities (limited public articulation):**

- VA AG has been active but less public than CA/TX/CO. Observed focus on privacy notice adequacy and processor contracts.
- 30-day cure period in effect.

**Practical implications:**

- VA enforcement has been moderate. The 30-day cure period currently provides operational cushion.
- Multi-state controllers should treat VA as established but not the lead concern.

## Oregon — OCPA + Data Broker Registry

**Enforcement infrastructure:** Oregon Department of Consumer and Business Services (data broker registry); Oregon AG (OCPA).

**Stated priorities:**

1. **Data broker registration.** OR DCBS published its first data broker registry in 2024 and pursued non-registrants.
2. **Specific third-party disclosure** under OCPA's right to know — operationally challenging for many controllers.

**Practical implications:**

- OR's data broker registry creates a separate compliance obligation distinct from OCPA applicability.
- OR's "specific third parties" disclosure requirement in OCPA is operationally significant — most controllers default to categories of third parties; OR uniquely requires names on consumer request.

## Multistate AG actions

**Pattern.** Several state AGs collaborate on large enforcement matters, particularly involving data breaches with multi-state consumer impact. Examples:

- *Multistate v. Blackbaud* (Oct. 2023): $49.5 million settlement, 49 AGs + DC (excluding California). Parallel FTC action announced February 2024 (no monetary penalty), finalized May 2024.
- Multistate breach actions occur regularly under state breach-notification and consumer-protection laws.

**Practical implications:**

- Large-scale incidents (especially breaches) trigger multistate AG coordination, which compounds settlement leverage.
- A controller settling with one AG should anticipate follow-on actions from other states.
- Cooperation with the lead AG often shapes the multistate outcome.

## FTC overlap

**FTC enforcement** under § 5 of the FTC Act (15 U.S.C. § 45) and the Health Breach Notification Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 318) overlaps substantially with state privacy enforcement. Recent FTC privacy actions:

- *In re GoodRx* (2023): $1.5M — pixel-based health-data disclosure.
- *In re BetterHelp* (2023): $7.8M — health-data sharing for advertising.
- *In re Avast* (2024): $16.5M — sale of browsing data after representing it would not be sold.

**Practical implications:**

- A controller with FTC exposure may face parallel state AG actions. The FTC consent decree often becomes a template for state demands.
- State sensitive-data definitions encompass much of what FTC treats as deceptive privacy practices, opening parallel theories.

## Industry-specific enforcement targeting

Patterns in 2023-2025:

| Industry | Enforcement focus |
|---|---|
| **Healthcare-adjacent / digital health** | FTC HBNR enforcement; state sensitive-data theories; CIPA pixel litigation; *Doe v. Meta MDL* |
| **Connected vehicles** | CPPA sweep; expanded privacy disclosure obligations |
| **Mobile gaming targeted at children** | Cal. AG + LA City Attorney *Tilting Point*; FTC COPPA + state minor-data theories |
| **Adtech and data brokers** | CA, OR data-broker registries; state sensitive-data theories; TX AG biometric / behavioral targeting |
| **Femtech / reproductive health** | Sensitive-data scrutiny intensified post-Dobbs; pre-CCPA *Glow* settlement is foundational |
| **Streaming / video platforms** | VPPA private class actions over Meta Pixel; coordinated plaintiff bar |
| **Telehealth / mental health** | FTC HBNR; sensitive-data theories; private MHMDA actions in WA |
| **Social media platforms** | TX AG + multiple states; minor-protection statutes; sensitive-data theories |

## Risk weighting in gap analysis

When scoring gaps for severity (per `references/workflows/gap-analysis-method.md`), incorporate AG posture:

- **Add 1 to severity** if the gap implicates a stated priority of a Tier 1 AG (CA, TX, CO).
- **Add 1 to likelihood** if the gap is in an industry currently under sweep / initiative.
- **Add 1 to severity** for matters likely to attract FTC or multistate attention (sensitive data, large breaches, deceptive privacy representations).
- **Subtract 1 from severity** for gaps in jurisdictions where cure periods remain in effect (operational cushion).

These adjustments are heuristic; document the rationale.

## How to use this reference

1. **Scope the engagement.** Which states are applicable? Which industries are in play? What data types?
2. **Cross-reference AG priorities.** For each applicable state, identify whether the controller's practices intersect any stated or observed priority.
3. **Prioritize remediation.** Practices that intersect Tier 1 AG priorities should be elevated regardless of statutory penalty cap.
4. **Calibrate disclosure / settlement strategy.** Where a controller has identified a likely violation, the AG's posture (cooperative vs. adversarial; rulemaking-focused vs. enforcement-aggressive) shapes the response strategy.
5. **Re-verify.** AG priorities shift. Before relying on this reference for high-stakes work, verify against current AG announcements.
