# Applicability Matrix — US State Consumer Privacy Laws

**Purpose.** A single comparison of the threshold tests, scope, and exemptions across every comprehensive state consumer privacy statute. Use this *before* any obligations analysis. If the entity does not meet a state's threshold, that state's substantive duties do not attach.

**Read this in conjunction with the relevant `states/<xx>.md`** for definitions, edge cases, and citations.

> **Methodology note.** Thresholds use *and* / *or* operators. A small change in interpretation (e.g., whether "consumers" includes one-time site visitors) can flip applicability. When in doubt, run the analysis with both readings and present both.

## How to use

1. Confirm the business profile inputs: entity type, jurisdiction, total annual revenue (most recent calendar year), distinct consumer count by state, sale/share/targeted-advertising practices, sensitive-data processing.
2. For each state below, evaluate the threshold and produce a verdict: **Applies** / **Likely Applies** / **Does Not Apply** / **Insufficient Info**.
3. For each "Insufficient Info" verdict, list the specific input needed.
4. Flag any "Likely Applies" results where the answer turns on a borderline number or interpretive ambiguity, and explain what tips it.

## How to compute "consumers"

Most state laws define "consumer" as a natural person who is a resident of the state, generally acting in an individual or household context. Notable variances:

- **California** counts natural persons who are CA residents acting in *any* context, including employees, applicants, contractors, and B2B contacts (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.140(i)).
- **All other comprehensive state laws** generally exclude individuals acting in a commercial or employment context (B2B and HR) from "consumer."
- **"Processed" or "controlled"** generally includes any data processing, not just sale or sharing. Even log files, analytics, marketing CRM, support tickets, and account data count toward consumer thresholds. Conservative counting must include all distinct natural persons whose PD is processed during the relevant period.
- **Solely-payment-transaction-data exclusion.** CT, OR, MT, DE, NJ, NH, MN, MD, RI exclude PD processed solely to complete a payment transaction from the consumer count. CA does not exclude this.

## Sectoral and entity-level exemptions — how to read them

A common mistake: assuming a HIPAA covered entity or GLBA-covered financial institution is wholly exempt. **Read the statute carefully — most states have moved from entity-level exemptions to data-level exemptions.**

- **Entity-level exemption**: Even if the entity processes data outside the sectoral framework, the entity is exempt entirely. Examples: VA, CT, UT exempt GLBA-covered financial institutions at the entity level.
- **Data-level exemption**: The sectoral data is excluded, but the entity must still comply with the state law for any other PD it processes (marketing, web analytics, employee data in CA, etc.). Examples: CO, OR, MT, DE, NJ, NH, MD, MN, RI exempt HIPAA PHI at the data level only.

When advising a sectoral entity, run the analysis twice: (1) does the entity-level exemption apply? (2) if not, what data is excluded vs. covered?

## Common "borderline" applicability scenarios

1. **Hovering near a threshold.** A business with ~95k–105k consumers in a state should be treated as applies-now if any growth is expected. Compliance build is multi-month; do not wait until the threshold is crossed.
2. **One-time visitors.** Whether a website visitor whose IP and device data are logged for analytics counts toward the consumer threshold is unsettled in most states. Conservative reading: yes. Aggressive reading: no, if the data is not "associated with" them in a way that allows identification or contact. Document the assumption either way.
3. **Cookie-only data.** Whether a business "processes PD" of state consumers solely by setting an analytics cookie on their browsers is fact-specific. The CA AG has taken expansive positions on cookie-derived identifiers as PI; Texas's interpretation under TDPSA is narrower in practice.
4. **B2B SaaS with no direct consumer relationship.** A B2B SaaS that processes its customers' end-user data is typically a *processor* — not a controller — and the consumer thresholds attach to the controller (the customer). The processor's direct duties under most state laws are limited to contract terms, security, and assistance. **Confirm whether any of the SaaS's own marketing or website data hits a state threshold separately.**
5. **Targeting analysis.** Most states require both presence in / business with the state AND certain consumer-volume or revenue thresholds. "Targeted to residents" is interpreted broadly: a national e-commerce site that ships to all states is generally targeting each state.

## Pending and likely-soon-effective laws

Several states have proposed or recently-enacted privacy laws not yet effective. When a question turns on one of these, flag the timeline and whether obligations will attach within the user's planning horizon. (Specific provisions evolve rapidly; verify against a current source.)

- **Pennsylvania, Massachusetts, Wisconsin, Michigan, North Carolina, Ohio**: bills repeatedly introduced; status varies.
- **Washington** (My Health My Data Act) — sectoral health data; not a comprehensive state privacy law, but bears similar duties for health data.
- **Illinois** (BIPA) — biometric-only, not comprehensive.
- **Other federal-level activity** (American Privacy Rights Act, Kids Online Safety Act, etc.) — not yet enacted; do not state as law.
