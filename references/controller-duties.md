# Controller Duties Comparison — US State Privacy Laws

**Purpose.** Side-by-side mapping of controller (and CA "business") obligations across comprehensive state privacy laws. Use this when running a gap analysis or building a remediation roadmap.

> **Operating note.** California uses *business* / *service provider* / *contractor* / *third party*. The other 19 use *controller* / *processor*. The terms are not identical; service providers and contractors have stricter contractual requirements than processors. Where this file says "controller," for CA also read the corresponding "business" obligation.

## Privacy notice — minimum content

Most state laws converge on a baseline list of disclosures. Differences are at the margin (CA is the most prescriptive). A privacy notice that includes the union of the items below generally satisfies all states.

**Universal baseline (all comprehensive state laws):**
1. Categories of PD processed.
2. Purposes of processing.
3. Categories of PD sold or shared / used for targeted advertising (and a method to opt out).
4. Categories of third parties with whom PD is shared.
5. Description of consumer rights and how to exercise them.
6. Mechanism to submit rights requests (typically two methods: web form and email or toll-free number).
7. Process to appeal a denied request (where appeal right exists).
8. Effective date and means to identify changes.
9. Contact information for the controller.

**California adds:**
- Categories of sources from which PD is collected.
- Categories of PI sold/shared/disclosed for a business purpose (subdivided).
- A "Notice of Right to Limit" describing the limit-use right for sensitive PI.
- Information on financial incentives and price/service differences (where offered).
- Annual disclosure metrics for large-volume businesses (≥10M consumers; CCPA Regs § 7102).
- Retention period for each category of PI.

**Colorado, Maryland (and similar)** adds:
- Statement of whether the controller engages in profiling that produces legal/significant effect.
- Where profiling is used, meaningful info about the logic, the categories of PD used, and the consequences.

**Connecticut** adds (effective 2025 amendments):
- Specific data minimization commitments.
- Heightened disclosures for known minors.

## Notice-at-collection vs. privacy notice

CA is the only state that imposes a separate **notice-at-collection** duty distinct from the privacy policy. The notice-at-collection must be provided at or before the point PI is collected, in plain language, listing categories of PI to be collected, purposes, retention, and links to the privacy policy and (where applicable) the "Do Not Sell or Share" and "Limit Use of Sensitive PI" pages.

In practice, many controllers satisfy this with a one-line in-form disclosure plus a link to the privacy policy. CCPA Regs § 7012 governs.

## Processor / service-provider contracts

All states require a written contract between controller and processor with prescribed terms. CA's requirements (CCPA Regs § 7051, § 7053) are the most prescriptive and include:
- Specifying the limited and specified purposes for which PI is processed.
- Prohibiting retention, use, or disclosure of PI outside the direct business relationship.
- Prohibiting combining PI with PI from another source (with narrow exceptions).
- Requiring the processor to notify the business if it can no longer comply.
- Granting the business audit rights.

Other states (VA, CO, CT, etc.) require similar but less prescriptive terms (purpose limitation, confidentiality, security, return/delete on termination, subprocessor flow-down, audit). A contract drafted to CA standards generally satisfies all states. The reverse is not true.

## Risk / data protection assessments

Most state laws (other than UT, IA, NE) require a controller to conduct an assessment for processing activities that present a heightened risk of harm. Triggering activities typically include:
- Sale of PD.
- Targeted advertising.
- Sensitive data processing.
- Profiling that produces legal or similarly significant effects.
- Activities involving heightened risk of harm.

Assessments must consider benefits and risks, mitigations, consumer expectations, and the context of processing. They must be retained and produced to the AG / regulator on request.

**California** has its own framework: the CPPA's risk-assessment regulations (finalized 2025) impose detailed assessment requirements, retention duties, and a duty to submit a summary to the CPPA on request. The CA framework is the most prescriptive in the country.

**Practical posture**: Adopt a single assessment template that satisfies the union of state requirements. A template aligned to CO regs and CA CPPA regs will generally cover all other states.

## Data broker registration (separate from comprehensive law)

- **California**: Cal. Civ. Code §§ 1798.99.80 et seq. — annual registration with the CPPA; fee; subject to the DELETE Act (Senate Bill 362) starting in 2026, requiring deletion via accessible mechanism.
- **Texas**: Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 509 — annual registration with the Secretary of State.
- **Oregon**: ORS § 646A.572 — registration with the AG.
- **Vermont**: 9 V.S.A. ch. 62 — registration with the AG; NOT a comprehensive privacy law, but data-broker-specific.

A "data broker" is generally defined as a business that knowingly collects and sells the PD of consumers with whom the business does not have a direct relationship. If the entity has any sale practices and any indirect-relationship consumers, run a data-broker analysis separately from the comprehensive privacy analysis.

## Strict data minimization — Maryland's standard

Maryland's MODPA imposes the strictest data minimization in the country: collection of PD is limited to what is "reasonably necessary AND proportionate" to provide or maintain a specific product or service requested by the consumer. This is materially stricter than the typical "necessary" or "necessary and proportionate" formulation.

Practical effect: a Maryland-targeted controller cannot collect PD for purposes the consumer has not specifically requested (e.g., generalized analytics, product improvement) without separate justification or consent. Privacy programs targeting MD compliance must reduce default data collection and tighten purpose specifications.

## Affirmative defense / written-program safe harbor

- **Tennessee TIPA**: Provides an affirmative defense to enforcement for entities that maintain a written privacy program reasonably conforming to the NIST Privacy Framework or comparable. The most explicit safe-harbor language in any state.
- **Other states**: No explicit safe harbor, though several states' enforcement practice considers good-faith compliance efforts in determining penalties.

For a multi-state program, drafting to NIST PF satisfies TN's defense and supports a defensibility argument elsewhere. Document the alignment.

## Cure periods — current state

Cure periods were a common feature at enactment but have largely sunset or were never universal.

| State | Cure period | Status |
|---|---|---|
| CA | 30-day cure under CCPA (sunsetted under CPRA 2023) | No general cure right; AG/CPPA discretion |
| VA | 30-day cure | In effect |
| CO | 60-day cure | Sunsetted Jan 2025 |
| CT | 60-day cure | Sunsetted Jan 2025 |
| UT | 30-day cure | In effect |
| TX | 30-day cure | In effect |
| OR | 30-day cure | Sunsetted Jan 2026 |
| MT | 60-day cure | Sunsetted April 2026 |
| Others | Vary; check `references/enforcement.md` | |

The trend is toward sunset, not extension. Treat cure periods as a temporary cushion at most, not a structural feature.
