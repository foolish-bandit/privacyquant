# Consumer Rights Comparison — US State Privacy Laws

**Purpose.** Side-by-side mapping of consumer rights across the comprehensive state privacy laws. Use this when (a) advising on what rights to support in a privacy program, (b) drafting consumer-facing rights disclosures, or (c) routing an incoming consumer request.

> **Caveat.** "Right exists" ≠ "right is identical across states." The mechanics, exceptions, response windows, and verification procedures vary. Use the per-state files for precise mechanics.

## Authorized agents and parental requests

- **California**: Permits authorized agents (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.135(c)). Agents must provide written authorization; the business may verify directly with the consumer. Agents are common in CA (e.g., Permission Slip, Mine).
- **Colorado, Connecticut**: Permit authorized agents for opt-out rights only (not access/delete). The CA-style model is *not* the universal default.
- **Other states**: Vary; some permit only the consumer to submit, except for parents acting on behalf of minors, or guardians for incapacitated persons.

When an authorized-agent request is received, route per the state of the consumer's residency, not the agent's.

## Sale, sharing, and targeted advertising — distinctions

This trio is the most-confused area in compliance work. Treat them as distinct.

- **"Sale"** (most states): exchange of PD for monetary or other valuable consideration. The "valuable consideration" prong is what catches many ad-tech disclosures that don't involve cash.
- **"Sharing"** (CA-only term): disclosing PI to a third party for cross-context behavioral advertising, whether or not for consideration. Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.140(ah).
- **"Targeted advertising"** (most states other than CA): displaying advertisements to a consumer where the ads are selected based on PD obtained from the consumer's activities over time and across non-affiliated websites or applications, used to predict consumer preferences or interests.
- **"Cross-context behavioral advertising"** (CA term): substantively similar to "targeted advertising" but with CA's own statutory definition.
- **"Profiling"** (most states): automated processing performed on PD to evaluate, analyze, or predict a consumer's economic situation, health, demographic characteristics, location, etc. Generally must produce decisions with legal or similarly significant effect to trigger opt-out.

A retargeting ad campaign using the Meta or Google pixel typically triggers:
- CA: "sharing" disclosure and opt-out requirement.
- VA, CO, CT, OR, MT, etc.: "targeted advertising" opt-out requirement.
- IA: no opt-out of targeted advertising (IA is the outlier).
- "Sale" classification varies: many state AGs and academic commentators treat ad-pixel data passing as "sale" because cookie/identifier exchange constitutes "valuable consideration." Conservative posture: treat as both sale and targeted advertising, and provide opt-outs for both.

## Universal opt-out mechanisms (UOOMs)

Where required by Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.135(b), 11 CCR § 7025, and Colo. Rev. Stat. § 6-1-1306(1)(a)(IV), the controller must recognize browser-/device-level signals — most prominently the Global Privacy Control (GPC) — as a valid opt-out of sale and (where applicable) sharing/targeted advertising/profiling.

**Implementation reality.** A controller subject to UOOM-recognition rules must:
1. Detect GPC at the page-load level (or equivalent for non-web channels).
2. Treat the signal as a valid opt-out without requiring sign-in or further verification.
3. Maintain the opt-out across the session and persist it for known users.
4. Reflect the opt-out in downstream ad-tech and analytics integrations.

## Sensitive data — opt-in vs. opt-out

The single biggest structural difference among the laws.

- **California**: No opt-in for sensitive PI. Instead, consumers have a "right to limit use and disclosure of sensitive PI" (CCPA Regs § 7027). The notice must explain this right and offer a "Limit the Use of My Sensitive Personal Information" link.
- **Utah, Iowa**: Opt-out structure (notice + ability to opt out). No opt-in.
- **All other states**: Opt-in required — the controller must obtain consumer consent before processing sensitive data. Failure to obtain consent before processing is a per-instance violation.
- **Maryland**: Opt-in plus a flat *prohibition* on sale of sensitive data and on processing for targeted advertising or profiling without consent. Treats sensitive data more strictly than any other state.

Sensitive data definitions vary in scope across Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.140(ae), Va. Code § 59.1-575, Colo. Rev. Stat. § 6-1-1303(24), Conn. Gen. Stat. § 42-515(28), Or. Rev. Stat. § 646A.570(17), and Md. Code Com. Law § 14-4601(cc). Common categories: racial/ethnic origin, religious beliefs, mental/physical health diagnosis, sexual orientation, citizenship/immigration status, genetic data, biometric data processed for unique ID, precise geolocation, data of a known child. CA also includes account credentials, contents of certain communications, government IDs, and (notably) consumer-disclosed financial information in some categories.

## Children and teens — overlapping rules

A non-exhaustive overlay:

- **Federal COPPA** for children under 13.
- **CA** (CCPA/CPRA): Sale/share opt-in for ages 13–15; parental consent for under 13. CA Age-Appropriate Design Code (AB 2273) — note: significant litigation history; check current status.
- **CT** (CTDPA amendments): Special protections for ages 13–16; default off for targeted advertising and sale of teens' data; data minimization for known minors.
- **CO**: Heightened protections for known children; profiling-of-minors restrictions.
- **MD**: Strictest treatment — flat ban on sale and targeted advertising of known minor data.
- **State social media laws** (CA SB 976, NY SAFE for Kids, FL HB 3, etc.) overlap. These are not comprehensive privacy laws but they affect privacy programs for minor-facing services. Note in the analysis when minors are present.
